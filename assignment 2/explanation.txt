I wanted to mess around with pixelation a lot. From the patterns and noise sections in the book of shaders, I liked seeing the grid-like structure.
When I added the pixelation, it reminded me of an old TV, so I tried to add a glitchy section based on mouse location. I ended up using the simple
sin-based noise generation algorithm for it and adding those values to the color's RGB values. I also used this as a way to get a bit more comfortable
with JS, as I don't have too much experience with it. The gulls implementation was pretty easy to use, though it was weird looking at my shader code
be a solid blue because of the JS character color in VSCode. I also wanted to figure out how to use the mod operator in WGSL as I had wanted to in
assignment 1, but couldn't find anything for it. Finally found something for it and got to use it to cycle through colors in the glitchy mouse
highlight.

The aesthetic I went for was an old TV with a glitchy spot. The resolution changes were fun to play around with, though no aesthetic intent was
directly behind them. The inverting colors was a fun addition I added to increase the glitchiness of the screen.

Feedback:
The mouse color adjustment wasn't bound to UV. I honestly didn't notice it until they pointed it out. They didn't pick up on the old-school TV, though
they did pick up on the glitchiness that the area around the mouse was supposed to give off. They also wanted more movement on the screen, which I don't
entirely disagree with, it just wasn't something I was super focused on. All the features seemed to work for them, as well as for me.
